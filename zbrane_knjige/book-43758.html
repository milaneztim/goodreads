In 1976, a uniquely seductive world of vampires was unveiled in the now-classic Interview with the Vampire . . . in 1985, a wild and voluptous voice spoke to us, telling the story of The Vampire Lestat.  In The Queen of the Damned, Anne Rice continues her extraordinary "Vampire Chronicles" in a feat of mesmeric storytelling, a chillingly hypnotic entertainment in which the oldest and most powerful forces of the night are unleashed on an unsuspecting world.

Three brilliantly colored narrative threads intertwine as the story unfolds:

- The rock star known as Vampire Lestat, worshipped by millions of spellbound fans, prepares for a concert in San Francisco.  Among the audience--pilgrims in a blind swoon of adoration--are hundreds of vampires, creatures who see Lestat as a "greedy fiend risking the secret prosperity of all his kind just to be loved and seen by mortals," fiends themselves who hate Lestat's power and who are determined to destroy him . . .

- The sleep of certain men and women--vampires and mortals scattered around the world--is haunted by a vivid, mysterious dream: of twins with fiery red hair and piercing green eyes who suffer an unspeakable tragedy.  It is a dream that slowly, tauntingly reveals its meaning to the dreamers as they make their way toward each other--some to be destroyed on the journey, some to face an even more terrifying fate at journey's end . . .

- Akasha--Queen of the Damned, mother of all vampires, rises after a 6,000 year sleep and puts into motion a heinous plan to "save" mankind from itself and make "all myths of the world real" by elevating herself and her chosen son/lover to the level of the gods: "I am the fulfillment and I shall from this moment be the cause" . . .

These narrative threads wind sinuously across a vast, richly detailed tapestry of the violent, sensual world of vampirism, taking us back 6,000 years to its beginnings.  As the stories of the "first brood" of blood drinkers are revealed, we are swept across the ages, from Egypt to South America to the Himalayas to all the shrouded corners of the globe where vampires have left their mark. Vampires are created--mortals succumbing to the sensation of "being enptied, of being devoured, of being nothing." Vampires are destroyed.  Dark rituals are performed--the rituals of ancient creatures prowling the modern world.  And, finally, we are brought to a moment in the twentieth century when, in an astonishing climax, the fate of the living dead--and perhaps of the living, all the living--will be decided.

OK, enough useless preamble. I reread The Queen of the Damned as part of my Halloween horror binge. I have long neglected the horror genre in favor of sci-fi, fantasy and even mainstream fiction. It never occurred to me to reread the first two Vampire Chronicles books Interview with the Vampire and The Vampire Lestat because I still remember the stories very well even decades after reading them (the Tom Cruise movie adaptation is even more fresh in my memory). The Queen of the Damned however, is only remembered in term of broad plot outline, and I the denouement totally escaped me. I think this is because there is so much in this book. It is more epic is scale and more complex in structure and characterization.

In the previous book The Vampire Lestat Lestat, the rebellious star of the Chronicles has become a rock star with hit albums (I think he made some kind of hair metal with weird lyrics). Soon she is dispatching young (or crappy) vampires left and right with her mental powers and human males in general are on her (s)hit list. Who can stop the most powerful vampire ever? I won’t spoil it for you, but it is probably not whoever it is you are thinking of.

There are long flashback chapters where the narrative is set in ancient Egyptian time where the human queen Akasha is turned into the first vampire almost by accident. This part of the tale involves good and evil spirits, cannibalism and curses, it really is quite riveting. The sections set in the modern world is almost as exciting, Anne Rice’s world building and vampire mythos is some of the most vivid fantastical creation ever. I particularly like the Talamasca, the secret society for investigation of the paranormal where Fox Mulder would feel right at home.

Anne Rice’s prose always go down well with me, I particularly like her description of the elation and shame of vampire feeding:

“When they drank the blood they felt ecstasy. Never had they known such pleasure, not in their beds, not at the banquet table, not when drunk with beer or wine. That was the source of the shame. It hadn't been the killing; it had been the monstrous feeding. It had been the pleasure.”

“Her skin was white and hard and opaque as it had always been. Her cheek shone like pearl as she smiled, her dark eyes moist and enlivened as the flesh puckered ever so slightly around them. They positively glistered with vitality.”

The Queen of the Damned is definitely worth rereading if you have read it ages ago like I have, of course if you have not read it before it is even more of an imperative though I would recommend reading the previous two books in the chronicles first. A great read from first page to last.

Review:
So, my review is once again… really long. I can’t help it! I have so many thoughts! I’m trying to at least divide these up as much as possible so that you all can choose which sections interest you and just read those if you don’t want to read the whole thing :-)

Things I Disliked/Things I Didn’t Mind but Others Might Dislike:

– A lot of the writing choices in this series are strange. For example, the book started off with Lestat breaking the fourth wall, explaining how he was going to tell us the story of what happened since the ending of the last book. And that was what made her even more awful to read about.

And really, she goes off into the wilderness and keeps to herself, not causing problems with anyone (other than those she kills, which I can accept since she needs blood to survive).

– Louis. I feel like a lot of people don’t like him, but I do. I like how human Louis is, and I too would be the one scolding Lestat for being too rebellious. I feel like we could get along.

Overall Thoughts:

Yes, there were some negatives to this book, mostly that it was slow with a lot of fluff, but I still think it was worth it. I think all three books so far have been worth it because these are some of the most unique and complex characters I have ever read about!

3.5/5

QUEEN OF THE DAMNED by Anne Rice is a bit of a mixed bag for me. I think of it as the weakest of the first four Vampire Chronicles but it's also something that displays the true breadth of the author's world-building and mythology. Perhaps too much (explained below). It also is a rather definitive ending for the series and the subsequent novels struggle to deal with the aftermath of the events.

Honestly, I feel like the best way to read this book is to start with the epilogue of THE VAMPIRE LESTAT. This is probably the only thing the movie got right as the ending of that book feels like the beginning of this one. What actually begins the book is a bunch of updates on other characters from the books like Armand, Louis, Daniel, Gabrielle, and Santino. I feel like it also establishes what a lot of fans truly wanted: Lestat and Louis mending their long-standing riff (for however long that lasts).

It's not really a spoiler to say the book deals with the rising of vampiric progenitor Akasha and her subsequent purging of her undead progeny. It's a shame as Anne Rice introduced a vast society of vampires, clubs, and secret rituals only to promptly incinerate the entire culture within a few chapters. A handful of Old Ones and younger undead are spared because of their connection to Lestat who, even then, is already the most important vampire in the universe.

Ironically, Lestat is not really the star of this book and is actually regulated to a minor supporting role for the most part. The real protagonists of this book are Maharet and Mekare. The fourth and fifth vampires in history respectively. Also, they're the people who accidentally created the vampire race and unleashed a good-looking but amoral plague on humanity.

The meat of the book is the lengthy flashback to ancient Egypt where the twin redheads (I'm not saying that's impossible in Africa but it's a little weird--so is vampirism I suppose, though) are a pair of seers that get involuntarily recruited to serve as spiritual consultants to the reigning Pharaohs. Cursed with an overabundance of honesty, the seers offend them repeatedly by stating that spirits are not gods and have no real care to the deeds of men. Everything more or less goes downhill from there.

The good parts of the book are very good with the establishment of the Talamasca (that would go on to inspire the Arcanum from Vampire: The Masquerade and Watchers from Highlander: The Series), the story of Jesse Reeves as she struggles to figure out what kind of monster her aunt is, and the gathering of the Old Ones as they struggle to give enough of a crap to stop the end of the world. Seriously, I actually kind of love that the vampires need a lot of coaxing to care to save the planet's human population.

The bad parts of the book more or less revolve around the fact Akasha is a supervillain only slightly more believable than Thanos. She's a petulant child in Kemet (Egypt), becomes a stone statue for a millennia, and then rouses from her slumber due to Lestat being a hard rock god. From there, she decides to eliminate 90% of all men from the world because they're the source of all evil. Amazingly, Anne Rice never pauses to mention how much evil Akasha has done but I'm going to assume this was meant to be self-obvious despite several vampires agreeing it's a solid plan.

The book also suffers from a number of deus ex machinas that aren't really necessary. Things such as a gathering of several thousand year old vampires, all of them individually weaker than Akasha, but including some that are only a few years at most younger than her. Vampires who are fully prepared to sacrifice their eternal lives to save half the human race and who are shaken from millennia of apathy for a confrontation with their dread mother. I assume you think that group and their decision will be vital to the resolution of the plot, right? Yeah, no.

Still, there's sparkles of genius here and there. Moments that feel tremendously real and tragic like Daniel's addiction/enslavement to Armand or David Talbot's temptation to become a vampire despite his advanced age. I mentioned that Jesse Reeves is a fantastic character in this book and one of my favorite parts. So much so that I actually am more angry about her treatment in the movie adaptation than I am Lestat's. A lot of good came in this book too, including obviously the Antediluvians and Gehenna from Vampire: The Masquerade. Anne Rice created a mythology here and it holds up well.

Unfortunately, Queen of the Damned has a lot of issues that drag it down for me. It's not just the plot holes above but also the fact that Anne kind of answers questions that never should have been answered. Much of what made the early Vampire Chronicles (okay, the first two books) so great was the existential crisis of not knowing where they came from or what purpose they served. The power creep here also more or less makes the vampires less like cursed undead and more like Superman with a taste for blood.
